01 The Problem

Coordination at scale without enforcement mechanisms.

ARC-Net ran across six research verticals with a team that was majority students and early-career researchers. High autonomy by design, low overhead by necessity.

As the organisation scaled, the gap between what was happening across verticals and what was visible to the founding team widened. Work logs went unfiled not because people weren't working, but because filing them felt like overhead with no return value for the person doing it. Briefs stayed vague because the cost of vagueness only showed up later, at review. Feedback from multiple collaborators arrived informally, contradicted itself, and left researchers to resolve the conflict alone. When someone moved on, what they knew moved on with them.

The question wasn't how to enforce better behaviour. It was how to make the right thing easier than the wrong thing, at every stage of the project lifecycle.

02 ARConnect

The platform I designed for this problem.

ARConnect was a full internal platform designed to systematise coordination across ARC-Net's five operational modules.

I designed the product requirements across work and productivity tracking, onboarding and offboarding, leave and compliance, team communication, and project visibility. A Phase 1 development proposal was scoped with an engineering partner, covering authentication, dashboard shells, work log submission, document upload, and leave management.

Building it fell outside what the team could take on at the time. The coordination problem remained, and the organisation kept growing. That's the gap this system addresses.

The design constraint

The platform vision was fully specified. The question became: what can be built right now, without engineering resources, that carries the coordination load until ARConnect exists? The answer shaped every decision in the system below.

03 The Diagnosis

Twenty workflow gaps. One split that drove the architecture.

Mapped across the full project lifecycle, from the moment someone joins to the moment a project closes.

The twenty gaps split into two categories once examined together. Some are conversational by nature: they need the right questions asked in the right order, produce a structured output, and require no persistent state, no access controls, no database. These are GPT-appropriate. The team member is the only person in the loop, and the output lives in a document they control.

Others require persistent records that survive beyond a single conversation, role-based access so the right people see the right data, cross-team visibility that aggregates across verticals, and audit trails for compliance. These are what ARConnect was designed to handle. Trying to substitute a GPT here produces something fragile that looks like infrastructure without being infrastructure.

That split drove the system design. Build well where GPTs genuinely fit. Mark everything else as platform-dependent and leave it there.

04 The System

A GPT layer mapped directly onto ARConnect's module structure.

Each GPT covers the conversational, automatable surface of one ARConnect module. The modules that need real infrastructure stay marked as platform-dependent.

The outputs connect across GPTs. The Translation Brief produced at planning becomes the reference the Retrospective closes against at project end. Daily logs become the raw material the weekly digest synthesises for leadership. The system shares a document language and output format without needing shared memory.

05 Decision Log

The forks, and what got rejected.

Fork 01 · Architecture
One monolithic GPT covering all workflows → Modular GPTs, one per workflow cluster

A single heavy prompt loses fidelity at the edges, and users don't know what to ask a general-purpose assistant. Modular also meant the team could adopt one workflow at a time, which is how it actually rolled out.

Fork 02 · Persistence
Google Sheets integration for log persistence → Stateless GPTs, production architecture documented separately

Converting a conversational tool into an infrastructure dependency means when the dependency breaks, the whole workflow breaks. Stateless was the more resilient choice for a system running without dedicated engineering support.

Fork 03 · Tone
Neutral, form-like interaction → Conversational, with a visible return for the person logging

Logs went unfiled because submitting felt like reporting upward with nothing coming back. Producing a personal reflection alongside the leadership summary gave the person logging a reason to complete it.

06 How the GPTs Worked

Three examples from the system.

Each GPT ran as a structured conversation with a defined output. A few examples of how that played out in practice.

Work and productivity module
Work Log

Asked four questions in sequence, then produced two outputs: a personal reflection for the team member and a structured leadership log. The flag field only populated if something needed escalation, which meant leadership got a signal without the person logging feeling watched.

Project execution module
Brief Intake

Walked the researcher through the Translation Brief conversationally and pushed back on vague answers until the scope and definition of done were concrete enough that two people would independently agree on them. It did not accept the first answer if that answer wouldn't survive a disagreement.

Review module
Feedback Synthesis

Took raw feedback from multiple reviewers in any format and produced a prioritised action note with each item tagged: Clear, Single Source, or Contested. Contested items surfaced both positions side by side. The GPT never resolved a conflict. That call belonged to the researcher and their lead.

07 What This Is Not

The GPT layer carried the conversational tier. The platform tier stayed open.

Fourteen of the twenty workflows identified remain platform-dependent. The GPT layer was never intended to close that gap.

Goal tracking, dependency flags across verticals, peer review routing, compliance records, cross-vertical project visibility, and hiring pipelines all require persistent infrastructure that a stateless GPT cannot provide without becoming something else entirely. The system map makes that boundary explicit and holds it there.
