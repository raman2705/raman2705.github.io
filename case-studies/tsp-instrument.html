01 THE PROBLEM

What selection couldn't see.

ARTPARK's Technology Innovators in Residence (TIR) programme selects early-stage deep-tech researchers at TRL 3 (lab-proven technology, no startup yet) and supports them through a structured arc toward commercialisation. Selection was application plus interview: well-suited to evaluating technical merit and idea quality, but without a structured way to assess the behavioural capacity to actually translate.

Translational work requires a specific set of things: pushing work into real deployment before it feels ready, revising direction when partners push back, staying attached to the problem rather than to a particular solution. Interviews surface how people talk about these things. Not how they navigate them.

The programme also needed something selection alone couldn't provide: a structured read on what each selected TIR would need from the programme once they were in.

One instrument. Two uses. Both from a single assessment taken at application.

02 THE DESIGN CHALLENGE

What does translational readiness actually look like?

The first problem was the construct. Translational readiness isn't a single trait. It's a cluster of behaviours that only become visible under specific conditions: when work is partially validated, when a partner contradicts your read, when the obvious move is to keep building rather than question the framing.

Standard personality assessments measure stable traits. This needed something closer to situational judgement: realistic scenarios with real trade-offs and no obviously correct answer, so the pattern of choices does the work, not any individual response.

Social desirability was the second problem. Researchers who've spent time in a translational programme know what "good" looks like in the abstract. The instrument had to make the right answer unclear in the moment, by embedding each decision in a scenario where every option has a cost.

This is also the instrument's defence against AI-assisted gaming. Applicants can paste a scenario into an AI engine and ask for the best answer. But the construct-correct option only becomes identifiable if you understand the specific failure mode each dimension is testing, and the options are calibrated so that reasonable, careful people would still make different choices.

03 CONSTRUCT ARCHITECTURE

Six dimensions. One underlying question.

Each dimension targets a distinct behavioural pattern that separates researchers who translate from those who don't. The underlying question across all six is the same: when things get hard in a specific way, what does this person actually do?

D1
First-Principles Thinking
Does the candidate question the framing, or only the solution?
D2
Applied Readiness
Can they ship and decide under partial validation, and name the cost?
D3
Resilience
Is failure treated as information, or as a threat to manage?
D4
Self-Direction + Collaboration
Can they decide alone when needed and loop others in without losing ownership?
D5
Founder Drive
Is intrinsic problem attachment paired with commercial urgency?
D6
Coachability
Does the candidate separate their identity from feedback about their work?

28 items total. Several dimensions carry sub-signals where within-dimension variation matters: D4 splits into self-direction and collaborative capacity; D5 splits into intrinsic drive and commercial urgency, both required. Imbalance between sub-signals is treated as meaningful, not noise.

Each dimension offers three parallel scenario tracks, developed in collaboration with programme leadership. The candidate picks the domain that feels most natural. This solves a real confound: a candidate unfamiliar with healthcare AI shouldn't score lower on first-principles thinking because the scenario felt foreign.

04 DUAL-OUTPUT DESIGN

One assessment. Two downstream uses.

The same scoring engine produces two outputs: a reviewer card for the interview panel, and a candidate report delivered to every applicant on portal submission, regardless of selection outcome.

The reviewer card is diagnostic: it directs interview attention toward the signal regions that need triangulation, not toward confirmation of the score. The candidate report is introspective: dimension bands are shown, but without the raw scores, flags, or selection implications visible to reviewers. The principle was that every person who completes the assessment should leave with something they can actually use.

Seven archetypes sit at the output layer, assigned through a deterministic decision tree. Each archetype carries a defined failure mode, which becomes the basis for what the programme should offer that TIR once they're in.

05 KEY DECISIONS

The choices that shaped it.

01
Situational judgement over trait measurement

Stable traits don't change under programme conditions. The instrument needed to capture behaviour in specific, high-pressure situations. SJT format forces a commitment to a course of action in a realistic scenario, closer to what the programme needs to predict, and harder to perform.

02
Costs on every option

Every choice carries a named positive attribute and an explicit cost. In translational work, every decision involves trade-offs. Candidates who can't see the cost of a "good" decision aren't ready for what the work actually demands. The format reflects that, not just tests for it.

03
Candidate report as a design requirement

The decision to deliver a substantive, honest report to every applicant on submission was made early and upstream. It changed what the instrument had to produce: not just a reviewer signal, but something a researcher could genuinely use regardless of what came next.

06 OPEN QUESTIONS

What deployment will answer.

The instrument has been through construct design, item writing, scoring calibration, archetype simulation across 2,000 synthetic profiles, and a full engineering specification. A small sample of real responses was also scored during development, which surfaced early questions about how the scoring behaves in practice. What it hasn't had yet is a full cohort.

The most important open question is predictive validity: whether archetype assignments and dimension patterns predict anything about how TIRs actually perform through the programme. The instrument is designed to be testable on this, but it can only be tested once there's outcome data to test against.

The AI-gaming concern sits alongside this. The instrument's construct depth is its main defence, but how much signal degrades under adversarial conditions is something only deployment data will show.

The first deployment is a signal-validation exercise. The instrument isn't being used as a hard selection cutoff. The priority is whether it produces meaningful patterns worth acting on, before that weight increases.
